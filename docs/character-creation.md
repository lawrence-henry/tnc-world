# Chapter 3: Character Creation {#character-creation}

> "Every Nomad starts with scars -- it's the ones you choose that define you."

The character you play in The Nomad Chronicles may appear to be a series of numbers and mathematical formulae on a sheet of power. What it is, however, is your eyes and ears into the world.

There are six steps to character creation, each one a straightforward extension from the step before it.

<!-- prettier-ignore -->
!!! info "Creation Steps Quick Reference"
    1. Choose a **Concept**
    2. Determine **Ability Scores**
    3. Select a **Background**
    4. Choose **Traits, Expertise, & Specializations**
    5. Pick or Purchase **Equipment**
    6. Finalize and Calculate **Derived Stats** (Health, Speed, Defense, etc)

---

## Step 1: Concept {#concept}

Every character starts as an idea. Who they are, what drives them, and what kind of survivor they're on track to become.

<!-- prettier-ignore -->
???+ tip "Think in Arcs"
    Before numbers hit the page, think personality, motivation, and aesthetic.

    - What keeps this character moving?

    - What broke them before the story began?

    - How do they fit with the group?

---

## Step 2: Ability Scores {#ability-scores}

Ability Scores are the backbone of how your character moves and operates through the world, and are used to determine your character's physical and mental prowess.

#### Determining Abilities

When you're ready, take your 3d6 and roll them, adding them together and comparing that result to the Determining Abilities Table. On the left side of that table are the various sum results of rolling 3d6. On the right side is where you'll find the Ability Scores you'll record on your sheet.

You'll need to make six total rolls. One per Ability. You may roll in order, starting from the top.

After rolling as standard, your GM may allow you to swap up to two Ability Scores of your choice, to better customize your concept.

<!-- prettier-ignore -->
??? tip "GM Tip"
    Optional Ruling: Like in certain dungeon-crawling systems, you could also simply allow your players to roll six times, record the results, and apply those results to whichever Ability Scores they fancy for specific build types.

##### Exceptional Start

: If, while rolling Ability Scores, a player rolls Doubles, they may add up to half of the value of their Chance Die to the result of their roll for determining their starting score.

: If they roll Triples, they may add up to the full value of their Chance Die to up to two different Ability Scores. No Ability Score can go above 6 at Character Creation in this way.

<!-- prettier-ignore -->
??? tip "GM-Tip (Scores above 5)"
    Any player that wants an Ability Score above 5 must first take at least 1 Infection Level to represent the Nano-Virus affecting that particular Score. 5 is peak human capacity.

##### Standard Array

Instead of leaving their characters in the hands of fate with a roll, a player may wish to start play with a Standard Array of Ability Scores. There are two options in this particular setup, depending on how specialized or versatile a player would like their character to be.

| Array 1 | Array 2 |
| :-----: | :-----: |
|   +4    |   +3    |
|   +2    |   +3    |
|   +2    |   +2    |
|   +1    |   +1    |
|    0    |    0    |
|   -1    |   -1    |

<!-- prettier-ignore -->
!!! note "Understanding High Scores"
    The pages below detail what having an Ability Score at certain values could mean for a character. A 0 is average, and is representative of any number of NPC capabilities. A 5 is peak capability for any human character, with any score above it counting as Super Human. No score can go above 5 without at least 1 point in Infection Level. A 10 is the game cap, and is a generally unheard of level of ability.

## Step 3: Background {#background}

The seven Backgrounds available represent a wide swath of personal or professional inclinations and training. They encompass many common personal experiences and walks of life. They are balanced based on what individuals in those professions sociotypically encounter and work with in their everyday lives. If your table requires more specific experiences or options, be sure to discuss the particulars with your GM.

Characters also do not generally start with much. Through playing, your character will have opportunities to improve their equipment but typically start with a few basics. Roll 2d6 and add your Background's RP Bonus for your starting RP amount. If you roll doubles, roll your Chance Die, and add it to the result. [See Chapter 7: The Armory, for more RP rules, and equipment.]

That said, with every Background is a list of possible starting benefits. A player may pick 1 of these benefits to go along with the other, automatic benefits of their Background. Optionally, the player may also roll 2d6 to randomly determine their starting benefit. The player may choose or roll another benefit at Levels 4, 8, 12, 16, and 20.

??? note "Optional Starting Traits"
    Need more flexibility in your background? Ask your GM about these optional Background Bonuses. Note that picking one of these replaces the Starting Benefit (at minimum) of the Background you've chosen.

    - Improv Expert: Once per Rest, pick a number of Traits you do not have equal to your Wits Ability, gaining a +2 bonus, as though they would in a Secondary Ability.

    - Student of All: Instead of gaining the default benefits of your Background, pick a single element from each of the available Backgrounds to start with.

    - Basic Training: Any time you would gain the Basic Tier of an Expertise, you may gain an additional Basic Tier of another Expertise.
