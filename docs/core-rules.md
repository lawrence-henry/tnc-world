# Chapter 2: Rules of Engagement

## Implements of Fate

---

These are the tools you will need for play.

### Players:

Players only need three things to be ready for play. With the right foreknowledge on the character concept you'd like to play, a character sheet can be completely filled out in as little as five to ten minutes. Which, with the brutality inherent in this system, could potentially be important.

1. Three six-sided die (3d6). Two of a single color :material-dice-6:, and a third of a contrasting color (or design):material-dice-6-outline:. We'll discuss why a little later in this chapter.
2. A pencil (or pen, if you like to live dangerously.) to adjust elements of your character.
3. A character sheet, to hold all of your character's items, weapons, and abilities for reference.

### Game Masters:

A Game Master (GM) won't need much more than their players. The GM, however, does have a lot more optional tools at their disposal.

**Needed:**

1. A physical or digital copy of this book.
2. At least 3d6. Again, two of a single color, and a third of a contrasting color (or design).
3. A single ten-sided die (d10).
4. An Adventure (a few sample adventures have been provided in Chapter 9 of this book. Feel free to mix, match, and mash-up to your heart's content).

!!! info "Optional"

    1. A GM Screen. Some prefer the secrecy and natural tension provided by the GM screen. Others let the dice fall where they may, for all to see, and let their player's demises be recognized before they announce them.
    2. A second ten-sided die (d10), should you desire to roll percentile chance situations, create/use d100 lists.
    3. Maps and/or minis.

## Abilities and How to Use Them

---

### A Brief Explanation of Abilities

There are six Ability scores, split between physical and mental prowess, listed below. The Average adult human's score is 0.

Throughout play you'll improve your skills, gaining Traits, Expertise, Specializations, and other abilities. Many of these modify the rolls you make with your Ability Scores, as bonuses and penalties.

Might {#ability-mig}
: This ability represents your character's physical strength. It covers such things as lifting heavy objects, carrying a friend, or grappling a foe.

Reflexes {#ability-ref}
: This ability represents your character's general dexterity and is used to determine how hard they are to hit, whether through speed or effective movement through cover.

Constitution {#ability-con}
: This ability represents your character's health, endurance, and resistance to the physical dangers of the world.

Wits {#ability-wit}
: This ability represents your character's acquired wealth of knowledge. It is useful in recalling information, planning, and understanding complex situations.

Willpower {#ability-wil}
: This ability represents your character's applied experiences. It is a key component in applying one's skills to varying situations.

Perception {#ability-per}
: This ability represents your character's senses and hand-eye coordination.

### Action Rolls

**3d6 + Ability + Trait**

Whenever you want to do something, aside from basic movement and conversation, your GM is likely to ask you to make an **Action Roll**. Everything else in the game derives from the **Action Roll**. From firing a weapon, to driving a vehicle, to finding that key you dropped under the refrigerator.

During play, your GM will set the scene and then open the floor to the players who decide how to interact with what’s been described.

Dice are only necessary when there is a significant consequence for performing (or failing) an action.

To make an **Action Roll**, roll your **3d6**. Two dice should be one color and the third a different color. The contrasting die is called the Chance Die. Add your relevant Ability Score, and an additional 2 if you have a related Trait, although the Trait is not necessary to attempt the roll. The final number is your test result, which you want to meet or exceed your Challenge Rating.

Even if you have several Traits that could apply, you can only use one on a test. You can never gain the benefits of more than one Trait on an **Action Roll**. These results are compared, either to a Challenge Rating set by the GM, or set by an opponent rolling against you.

#### Making an Action Roll {#action-roll}

When it is time to make a test, you are either rolling against a fixed Challenge Rating (CR) determined by the GM based on assumed difficulty, or an opponent's roll.

1. You tell the GM what you are trying to do.
2. The GM determines the ability and applicable Trait (if any). They then assign a CR to the task based on its difficulty and the prevailing circumstances.
3. You make an **Action Roll** and figure out your result.
4. If your test result is equal to or greater than the CR, you have successfully completed your action as intended.

### Advantage and Disadvantage

At times, your GM may decide a roll has been particularly affected by your circumstances. At that time, they will inform the player that their character has a minor or Major Advantage or Disadvantage. Following are the bonus(es)/penalty(ies) and examples.

2 or more sources of Minor Advantage/Disadvantage becomes a Major Advantage/Disadvantage. On the other hand, regardless of number, Major Advantages/Disadvantages do not stack in this way, although multiple instances and sources count when determining whether or not an Advantage or Disadvantage cancels its opposite out.

**Minor Advantage/Disadvantage:** +2 (2-on-1, target flanked, climbing using a rope)

**Major Advantage/Disadvantage:** +5 (3 or more on 1, target unaware, climbing using a ladder)

<!-- prettier-ignore -->
!!! tip "Things to remember about Action Rolls"
    - Only one Trait can apply to a Action Roll.
    - Leave the dice on the table after a roll because the result of the Chance Die may be important.
    - In Opposed Action Rolls, ties are broken by the higher of the total of the 2 regular dice, by the Chance Die after, or by the higher ability if the Chance Dice are tied. If all else fails, the tie will go to the defender of the action.
    - For Basic Action Rolls, the GM needs to assess the situation and assign a difficulty.
    - Advanced Action Rolls use a success threshold to measure completion over time, or as a rough estimate of the effective Threat Level of non-combat situations.

### Chance Pool

As mentioned above, your off-color die is called the Chance Die. The number of Chance Points you receive is usually equal to the number that comes up on your Chance Die.

When doubles are rolled on certain types of tests, this generates **Chance Points**. These points go into a pool, to be used to fuel various special maneuvers called Chance Actions.

A Player Character's Chance Pool starts at **6** Chance Points at the beginning of every session, and caps at **18**. Unused points are lost at the end of the session.

## Combat

---

When diplomacy breaks, and brutal force is the only outcome, the solution may be at the end of a barrel. For the inexperienced, this is a path to glory. For the seasoned, survival is victory enough. Yet, nothing can prepare you for the chaos of the battlefield: the deafening roar of gunfire, the thunderous shock of grenades, and the desperate cries of the wounded. In this unforgiving arena, you'll have to fight to win. To survive.

Combat is a cornerstone of The Nomad Chronicles, defined by intense and decisive moments. At its heart of course lies the **Action Roll**, but when lives hang in the balance, more is required. You'll need to understand how attacks are made, how damage is dealt, and what it takes to push a character to and beyond their limits.

### Initiative {#initiative}

Eventually, tensions will spike, and it will be important to make sense of what could be a chaotic battlefield. That's when the GM will tell the players to "Roll for Initiative."

<!-- prettier-ignore -->
!!! example "Initiative Roll"
    **Initiative = Reflexes + 3d6 + Modifiers**

Once Initiative has started, time is tracked in 15-second increments called rounds. When the GM calls on you for your turn in the Initiative, you describe the various actions you'd like to attempt and roll for them. Once everyone has taken a turn, the round ends and a new one begins, until the end of the scenario that made Initiative necessary.

<!-- prettier-ignore -->
!!! example "Time Conversions"
    1 round = 15 seconds

    4 rounds = 1 minute

### Taking Actions

When it’s your turn in a round, you tell the GM what you want to do. Below are listed various actions that can be taken. The list is broken down into the least involved Non-Actions to the all-important Major Actions.

Unless otherwise stated, you can only perform one of each type of action in a round. You are not limited to the listed actions, but must discuss with your GM if what you'd like to do doesn't fall into one of these categories.

#### Non-Actions

Non-Actions are activities that are done passively, don't require notable time or focus to perform, and in general consume no resources on your turn. These are often reflexive or incidental actions that flow within other actions, such as moving while speaking, or scanning the environment while standing guard.

MOVE:
: You can move up to your Speed in yards. You can also go prone, stand up, enter or exit a vehicle, but if you do so you can only move at half Speed (rounded down). If you start a round prone and do not stand up, you may only crawl, moving at half Speed.

PERCEPTION CHECK:
: You can take a quick glance about, or listen passively in your environment without focusing on a specific detail.

SHOUT!:
: You can make a brief, simple communication (such as a shout, warning, or command) of five words or less.

CHANCE ACTION:
: You can use a Chance Action as an extension of a Major Action, unless otherwise described.

#### Instinct Actions

Instinct Actions are swift, immediate responses to another character's actions, allowing you to act out of turn under specific conditions.

ATTACK OF OPPORTUNITY:
: If a target moves out of or through your melee reach, you can make a single melee attack roll against them.

INTERCEPT:
: If an ally within 2 yards of you is attacked, you may attempt to move between the attacker and ally, redirecting the attack to yourself.

QUICK DRAW:
: If equipped with a Quick-Draw Holster or similar, you can draw a weapon as an Instinct Action when you spot an opponent, letting you react to surprise encounters without delay.

#### Free Actions

Free Actions are actions that you can perform on your turn without requiring significant time or focus.

QUICK DROP:
: Instantly release a held item, freeing up a hand.

SHIFT GRIP:
: Change your grip on a weapon (e.g., switching from one-handed to two-handed or vice versa).

SIMPLE GESTURE:
: Make a basic signal to an ally, such as pointing to a location or signaling to take cover. This could also be a spoken statement of about 25 words or less.

#### Minor Actions

Minor Actions are small but significant movements aimed at maneuvering skillfully around the battlefield.

ACTIVATE:
: You must use this action to start using certain Traits, such as those with the Style tag. This action also allows you to use items.

AIM:
: You take a moment to align your next strike. If your next action is an attack, you gain a Minor Advantage (+2) bonus on your next attack roll.

JUMP:
: You can jump from a standing or moving position. A standing high or long jump has a total distance of your Might in yards. A jump while you're moving has a total distance of your Might + Reflexes in yards.

PREPARE:
: You pick one major action that you're prepared to use, and what will trigger it, and then end your turn. Any time until your next turn, you can interrupt another character and take your prepared action immediately as an Instinct Action. If you don’t use it by your next turn, the action is lost. You can only take the Prepare action if you haven't already taken a major action.

READY:
: You draw or holster a weapon or ready an item that was otherwise stowed for quick access.

HOLD POSITION:
: You decide you won't be moved, planting yourself firmly. As long as you have not Moved, you can maintain Hold Position with a Minor Action for a number of Rounds equal to your Might (Strength). Any enemy who uses the Push-back or Knock Prone Chance Actions must succeed in an opposed test pitting their Might (Strength) vs. your choice of your own Might (Strength) or Reflexes (Acrobatics) in order to succeed.

When your opponent attempts to move you, should you win, the Chance Points they spent are still expended to no effect, regardless of how many Chance Points they spent in an attempt to move you (such as to move you more than 2 yards in a single attack with the Push-back Chance Action).

RUSH IN:
: You may move up to half your Speed (round down) in yards and then use a single Major Action. If this action is a melee attack against an adjacent enemy, you gain a +2 bonus on your melee damage total.

TAKE COVER:
: Taking cover can be a Non-Action as part of your Initiative. Afterwards, it becomes a Minor Action if suitable cover (or a nearby object or individual) becomes readily available. The type and extent of cover provide varying levels of protection, but remember, cover might not always be practical or accessible. Choose your cover wisely, as a bullet might still have your name on it.

1/2 Cover:
: Gain a +2 bonus to Defense and Reflexes Saving Throws.

3/4 Cover:
: Gain a +5 bonus to Defense and Reflexes Saving Throws.

<!-- prettier-ignore -->
!!! quote
    "They don't need to spot you to kill you, if they've got you cornered. Use cover to survive, but don't expect it to hide you forever." -- Captain Gil Thomas, Nomad Tactical Team 05-Bravo

Total Cover:
: You cannot be targeted by ranged attacks or abilities unless the attacker changes position or has a method to detect and bypass the cover.
: While in Total Cover, you may make a **Blind Fire** attack without exposing yourself. This attack suffers **Major Disadvantage (-5)**, and **cannot use Chance Actions or Chance Breaks**. However, you may still benefit from the **Chance Die**, representing a lucky or instinctual hit.
: Attacking from Total Cover allows enemies to **guess your position**. If you did **not Move** when taking cover, an attacker may roll a **Perception (Empathy)** check vs your **Reflexes (Stealth)**. On a success, they may attempt a single attack against you as though you had **3/4 Cover**.

For detailed rules on using items and individuals as cover, refer to Chapter 6: The Armory.

<!-- prettier-ignore -->
??? tip
    In order to use an individual as improvised cover, the aggressor must first be in control of a grapple. The target character must be Entangled or Helpless in order to be used as cover. If an attack is made, and would miss the controlling character by 5 or less points, the attack hits the hostage used as cover, dealing normal damage to them.

#### Major Actions

Major Actions are substantial, requiring the majority of your focus and time within a turn, like attacking or interacting with complex objects.

AID:
: You take a moment to assist an ally, granting them a Minor Advantage (+2) on a single Action Roll (combat or ability).

BLIND FIRE:
: You throw caution to the wind, firing at your target without fully assessing their position. You suffer a Major Disadvantage (-5) to the attack roll, and cannot use Chance Actions or Chance Breaks with the attack.

DEFEND:
: You brace yourself for incoming attacks, focusing entirely on self-preservation. Until the start of your next turn, you gain a +2 bonus to your Defense.

GRAPPLE:
: You make a Melee Attack roll against an adjacent enemy. If you succeed, you and your target then engage in an opposed Might Action Roll. Success in this test grants you control of the grapple, applying the Entangled Condition to your target. While you control the grapple, you may:

1. Choke: Invoke an additional opposed Might Action Roll. On a success, target gains 1 Tier of the Winded Condition. A choke can be held using a Minor Action at the start of each new turn the target hasn't escaped. Each turn the choke is held increases the Winded Condition by 1 Tier.

2. Strike: Attack your target with an Unarmed attack or a light, one-handed weapon you possess or can draw.
   : Targeting the Entangled Character: The attack is made with a Minor Advantage due to the close proximity. The penalty for Called Shots is reduced by one step (from Major Disadvantage to Minor Disadvantage, or from Minor Disadvantage to a standard roll).
   : Targeting the Completely Restrained Character: The attack is made with a Major Advantage. The penalty for Called Shots is eliminated, and the attack is more likely to hit due to the target's vulnerability.

3. Lock: Invoke an additional opposed Might Action Roll. On a success, the target gains an additional Tier of the Entangled Condition.

4. Drag: Invoke an additional opposed Might Action Roll. Success here allows you to travel up to half of your Movement Speed while maintaining the grapple. This Action Roll is automatically successful on a Subdued target.

5. Ranged Attack: While grappling, you can use a Major Action to make a Ranged Attack (not affected by Expertise or Expertise) against your target or another enemy within range.
   : Targeting the Entangled Character: The attack is made with a Minor Advantage due to the close proximity. The penalty for Called Shots is reduced by one step (from Major Disadvantage to Minor Disadvantage, or from Minor Disadvantage to a standard roll).
   : Targeting the Completely Restrained Character: The attack is made with a Major Advantage. The penalty for Called Shots is eliminated, and the attack is more likely to hit due to the target's vulnerability.
   : Targeting Another Character: The attack is made with a Minor Disadvantage (-2) due to the difficulty of aiming accurately while engaged in close combat.

6. Throw: Invoke an additional Might Action Roll. Success here throws your target a number of yards equal to half your Might score, dealing damage based on the AP value of whatever they hit.

Both you and your target suffer a -2 penalty to Defense while grappling. If the target is Helpless, they suffer a -5 penalty to Defense.

Each round, the Entangled character can attempt to break free or take control of the grapple by repeating the opposed Might Action Roll.

HEAL:
: You provide some quick first aid to an injured ally. You must be adjacent to your ally and you must have bandages ready. See the Healing section below for specific rules regarding Heal Action Rolls.

<!-- prettier-ignore -->
!!! warning "Failed Heal Tests"
    Failing a Heal Action Roll can unfortunately make a situation much worse. 
    If the Action Roll is failed by 2 or more, the target of the action endures a Minor Complication, gaining a Condition appropriate to the first aid attempted. 
    If the Action Roll is failed by 5 or more, the target of the action endures a Major Complication, losing an amount of Health equal to the Healing character's Chance Die, and gaining 2 Conditions appropriate to the first aid attempted. 
    A failed Heal check can be retried, either until the target character is dead, or the Healing character succeeds, which then applies the Successful Heal Action Roll results.

<!-- prettier-ignore -->
??? example
    Theo moves to his wounded ally, pulling bandages from his medic's bag. His ally has been shot, and he's got to work fast to get the bullet out. Theo, unfortunately, gets a 9 total for his Heal Action Roll, failing the roll.

    The GM decides, based on what he was attempting, that the most appropriate condition in this situation is a level of the Bleeding Condition.

    Theo attempts a second time. This time, he rolls a 5, failing the roll exceptionally. His ally takes 3 unresisted Piercing Damage, another level of Bleeding, and Nauseated Conditions, as Theo was a bit too far off with his knife placement in attempting to extract the bullet.
    Taking a second to breathe, calming himself from the stress, Theo tries a third time, this time succeeding with a 14.

    The ally gains the benefit of the specified Heal action, and can no longer benefit from another Heal Action Roll until taking additional damage or resting.

MELEE ATTACK:
: You attack one adjacent enemy (within 2 yards) with your fists, boots, or a hand.

RANGED ATTACK:
: You fire or throw a missile weapon at one visible enemy within range.

<!-- prettier-ignore -->
!!! note "Near Miss" 
    In combat, some attacks may fall just short of hitting, but still have an effect on the target. If an attack roll misses, but is within 2 points of a character's Defense, the attack technically hits, but does the minimum amount of damage possible, to a minimum of 1."

SPRINT:
: You can move up to double your Speed in yards. You cannot take this action if you are prone (you’d need to use the Move action to stand up first).

#### Attacks & Damage

Combat revolves around two primary attack types: Melee and Ranged, both resolved the same way:

1. **Choose a Target.** Melee attacks require you to be adjacent (within 2 yards) of an opponent. Ranged attacks require line of sight and must be within your weapon's range.
2. **Roll to Attack.** Make an **Action Roll** using the appropriate Ability for your weapon (e.g., Might for Archaic Melee, Perception for Firearms), modified by Traits, Expertise, and/or other relevant bonuses.
3. **Compare Results.** Your attack roll must meet or exceed the target's Defense to hit.
4. **Calculate Base Damage.** On a successful hit, you deal an amount of Damage equal to Weapon Damage + Ability Score.
   **Chance Damage (On Doubles).** Whenever you roll Doubles on an attack, add your Chance Die to your **Base Damage**.
   **Critical Damage (on Triples).** Whenever you roll Triples, add twice your Chance Die to your **Base Damage**.

#### Death & Dying

- When a PC's Health reaches 0, they begin dying, losing consciousness and perishing in 2 + Constitution in rounds unless healed. They can speak on their turn, but take no actions.

- Most NPCs die instantly at 0 Health, though GMs can allow PC rules for significant foes at their discretion.

- Health does not drop below 0 --- there are no negative Health values.

**Pulling Killing Blows**

- If an attacker wishes to incapacitate instead of kill with an attack that would leave a target at 0 Health, they can declare a non-lethal attack. For any weapon that does not deal bludgeoning damage, this attack is made at a Major Disadvantage (-5). A successful attack leaves the target at 1 Health, regardless of damage total, and the target suffers Stunned (T5) for 1 minute (4 rounds) or until healed.

**Delivering a Coup de Grace**

- A **Helpless**, **Unconscious**, or **Dying** character can be executed as a Major Action. The attacker forces a special Saving Throw (TN 10 + Base Damage).

- If the target is Helpless or Unconscious but not already Dying, failure reduces them to 0 Health and they begin Dying. Success leaves them at 1 Health but Unconscious for a duration rolled on the Downtime Chart, regardless of healing.
- If the target is already Dying, they automatically fail and are killed outright.

#### Healing

Conversely, when a character is wounded, there are several ways to be healed.

Immediate Care:
: Another character can render immediate aid as a Heal action, as described previously in this chapter. The attempting character must roll a Healing (Wits) Action Roll CR 10. Immediate Care must be applied within 1 minute (4 Rounds) of an injury occurring to be effective. You can only benefit from Immediate Care once per Rest. A successful roll on Immediate Care recovers your Constitution in Health.

First-Aid Healing:
: You or another character can aid you with a Heal action, as described previously in this chapter. You can only benefit from First Aid twice per Rest. The attempting Character must roll a Healing (Wits) Action Roll CR 12. A successful First-Aid Healing roll recovers an amount of Health equal to the attempting character's Level.

Surgery:
: You or another character can aid you with a Heal action, as described previously in this chapter. Note that attempting a Surgery test on yourself is done with a Major Disadvantage (-5) to the roll. The attempting character must roll a Healing (Wits) Action Roll CR 15. A successful Surgery roll adds the attempting character's Wits and your Constitution to your next Rest's recovery amount. Failure deals damage to the target equal to what the recovery would have been. Surgery is one of the only ways to heal certain Conditions.

Breather:
: You can take a 5 to 10-minute break after a combat encounter. After a breather, you get back 2 + Constitution in Health. The benefits of a Breather only occur once after an encounter's end, and not at all if you ended the encounter at 0 Health.

Rest:
: You take 6-10 hours of minimal activity, focusing on your own recovery. If you can get at least 6 hours of rest, you heal 5 + Constitution in Health.

Professional Medical Care:
: Being cared for in a professional medical environment grants significant benefits to healing. Most notably, a +2 bonus to Health recovery for every Rest.
