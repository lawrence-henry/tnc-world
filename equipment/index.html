    Chapter 7: The Armory¶

    Characters will need equipment to survive — ranging from mundane items to advanced experimental gear. This chapter discusses the tools, armor, and weapons you'll encounter throughout The Nomad Chronicles.

    Common Items¶

    Common modern items like smartphones, shoes, and jackets are considered a part of everyday life and typically don't require tracking unless they're relevant to a particular scenario. If characters are in an extraordinary survival situation or a heavily restricted zone, GMs may rule to limit or remove access to Common Items as the narrative requires.

    Likewise, it's not exactly necessary to keep up with a lot of common consumables, such as gasoline, scraps, and food; except when characters aren't in a place with easy access to those items.

    Requisition Power¶

    By virtue of profession, or by completing objectives and missions, your character is awarded with finances, contacts, and influence. Requisition Power reflects a combination of those factors.

    Instead of tracking exact currency values, equipment in this game is acquired using Requisition Power (RP), a measure of your character's influence, connections, and resources. RP is gained through Background, Mission Rewards, and Downtime Work.

    RP Examples
    Example 1:
    Dash needs to capture and contain a high-value target. He needs a rifle, ammunition, and a set of handcuffs. These items should be provided by the organization assigning him. His RP could represent his rank and approved paperwork turned into his superiors, pursuant to his mission.
    Example 2:
    Theo needs to collect samples from a nearby biological weapons facility. But, as this is a personal project, he has to provide the materials himself. Using RP in this case could represent connecting phone calls, licensing fees, and/or purchasing bits and pieces of kit from a local vendor.

    At the start of the game, and upon leveling up, players roll 2d6 and add their Background's RP Bonus. If doubles are rolled, the Chance Die is rolled, and the result is added to the RP total, before any further calculation, such as from the Leadership Expertise (See Expertise). Players are guaranteed a minimum amount equal to their Background's RP Bonus regardless of the roll.

    Players also gain this RP bonus at the beginning of every in-game month, as long as they use at least some downtime to perform in their particular background field. Players enrolled in a program like the Nomad Initiative are provided Military Background RP as their monthly pay, regardless of their personal background.

    GMs are encouraged to provide RP rewards as part of the game. Most often, this is going to involve payments and an increase in influence for completing missions or assignments.

    Mission Payments

    2d6 x Threat Level

    • GMs seeking to make adjustments to this amount should consult Chapter 8: GameMaster's Notes.

    RP can be used during downtime to purchase gear, vehicles, weapons, etc. At the end of every in-game month, an amount of RP is deducted for living expenses and any leased or rented items.

    Active Inventory¶

    A person can only carry so much. A character's carry capacity is determined by their Might Ability.

    Active Inventory refers to the amount of gear a character can store on their person in a ready, mission-accessible fashion. Inventory is measured in Units, where each Unit represents a manageable amount of space and weight.

    If a character carries more Units than their Inventory Capacity allows, they become encumbered. For every 1 Unit (or every 25 lbs, if using weight tracking) beyond the limit, the character gains 1 tier of Fatigue per minute of sustained movement or activity.

    Encumbrance is cumulative and dangerous, especially in hostile environments. Excess gear should be stowed or managed with caution.

    Untrained? Why Wait?¶

    As described in Chapter 4, some equipment requires formal training. This applies to Weapons, Armor, and other specialized equipment. Characters that are considered untrained gain only limited benefit from the item in question.

    Untrained with Armor:
    Gain only half of the item's Defense and Armor Protection (AP).
    Cannot apply bonuses granted by Constitution through Armor Training Traits.
    Untrained with Weapons:

    May only make attacks using the appropriate Ability Score (Might, Reflexes, or Perception).

    Do not add an Ability Score to base damage.

    May only benefit from circumstantial bonuses (e.g., flanking, elevation, surprise).

    Training is the key to mastery. Without it, survival becomes that much harder.

    Living Expenses & Lifestyles¶

    A character's lifestyle affects their narrative comfort, social standing, and access to basic items. Higher lifestyles offer assumed perks and access to quality-of-life items, while lower ones may limit options.

    Lifestyle Monthly Cost
    Squalid: 0 (Free)
    Impoverished: 10 RP
    Modest: 20 RP
    Comfortable: 30 RP
    Wealthy: 40 RP
    Aristocratic: 50 RP
    Squalid¶
    Little to no expenses. Characters in this lifestyle are often homeless or living on scraps with bare-bones living. Likely sharing rooms, eating the cheapest possible food, and have minimal liquid resources for luxuries, such as electronic devices and transportation.

    Note

    This is the only lifestyle in which the Common Items discussed at the beginning of this Chapter are not guaranteed. GMs are encouraged to price these items appropriately, so that if a character is able to save up, they'll have opportunity to begin buying these basic items.

    Impoverished¶
    A slight step up in quality of life. Likely to still be sharing an apartment or townhouse, but still struggling to get by and relying on public transportation.
    Modest¶
    The average working adult, able to afford a furnished apartment, decent food, and some level of leisure or convenience, such as a car or entertainment devices.
    Comfortable¶
    More disposable income. A PC here would likely have a better home to live in, such as a small house or luxury apartment. They likely have luxuries such as a better car or travel options, and more expensive tastes in food and entertainment.
    Wealthy¶
    High-end living, premium real estate, multiple vehicles, fine dining, vacations, etc.
    Aristocratic¶
    Mansions, private planes, hired help, luxury everywhere. These are extremely high-end consumers, and the sky is the limit on cost.

    Warning

    If a player isn't careful, they can overextend themselves, financially. The guidelines in this note are only applicable if the campaign goes on with enough time to justify it. PCs are paid at the beginning of each in-game month, and pay for their Living Expenses at the end of the month.

    Paul has lived a Comfortable Lifestyle for a while, but occasionally has a bit of an overspending habit. He decides that, for his birthday, he's going to buy and modify an upgraded rifle. He ends up spending far too much, leaving only 50 RP for living expenses. At the end of the month, his Comfortable Lifestyle is downgraded to modest living conditions. Several leased or rented items are repossessed, but it's unfortunately not enough to cover the difference. Either way, the financial strain is evident, and can potentially become a larger problem if left unchecked.

    Expensive Items¶

    Some items simply can't be bought or requisitioned outright, without the right amount of money and/or connections. These items often come with payment plans, or leases, in which the customer pays a monthly amount to (eventually) own the item.

    Any item of 30 or higher RP, at the GM's discretion, can possibly be broken down into monthly payments of RP, the amount of which is decided on at the time of purchase.

    Info

    Example: A Forced Entry Unit armor costs 583 RP (representing ~$17,490 or so). A character wishing to own/use this item could make an agreement for 6.5-7 RP/month for 90 months, or 49 RP/month for 1 year.

    Armor¶

    Armor provides additional protection from incoming damage. It is known by the term Armor Protection, or AP. AP is used to determine how much damage gets through to your body, how heavy the armor is, and how it affects your movement speed.

    Much like weapons, covered later in this chapter, armor has been mostly abstracted for ease of play. Modifying to represent specific brands or armor systems should be discussed with your GM.

    Armor Training:
    Like with weapons, a character must be familiar with their armor in order to get the most out of using it. Armor training is tracked in levels similarly to an Expertise. Each level indicates how much of your Constitution can be applied as a bonus to your Armor Protection (AP).
    Armor Training Levels Constitution Bonus
    Basic +1/2 Con
    Initiate +Con
    Advanced +Con +1
    Expert +Con +2

    Defense & Speed

    Defense
    How hard it is to get hit. Determined by 9 + Reflexes + Armor Defense Bonus.
    Armor Defense Bonus:
    Your Armor Defense Bonus is determined by your armor's weight. Light Armor grants a +2 Bonus, Medium Armor grants a +3 Bonus, and Heavy Armor grants a +5 Bonus.
    Armor Protection:
    How hard it is to get hurt from being hit. Determined by Constitution + AP
    Speed:
    How fast your character moves in a turn of combat. Speed is determined by your Reflexes ability, and modified by your Armor Protection. Starting at 0, a character can move 10 yards per turn, increasing or decreasing that movement speed by 2 yards per turn. A character with 0 Speed cannot move, and automatically fails Reflex Saving Throws.

    Wearing armor provides a static penalty (based on the AP of the armor) to your Ability for determining speed. For example, a police officer with a Reflexes of +2 can normally move at 14 yards per turn. Wearing a Light-Duty Vest (AP 5), however, makes his Reflexes count as a -3 for movement purposes, making him move at 4 yards per turn due to the encumbrance of the armor.

    A lot of the armor and weapons are labeled somewhat generically, and given the properties they are likely to have on average.

    If you or your GM would like to be even more specific, however, feel free to discuss and alter things; for, as we all know, no two pistol or rifle brands are the exact same. If more options are necessary for your table to have fun, put properties together to make more specific weapons. GMs are encouraged to have an idea for adjusting the cost of their weapons to reflect said specificity.

    Leather Jacket¶

    Armor Protection: +2, Cost: 5 RP

    A leather jacket, possibly with sewn-in decoration. Provides a Minor Advantage on Saving Throws against the Hypothermic Condition. Provides an additional +1 bonus to Defense against Called Shots aimed at the Chest, Heart, or Vitals/Gut. Does not count as armor for the purposes of AP or HP rounds.

    Civilian/Covert Armor¶

    Armor Protection: +2, Cost: 10 RP

    A light, discreet set of armor that offers some protection without looking like military or tactical gear. Provides a +1 bonus to social checks involving diplomacy. Emphasizes mobility over protection.

    Light Undercover Shirt¶

    Armor Protection: +5, Cost: 15 RP

    A shirt made of bullet resistant material. The fibers are woven to ensure even dispersal of energy when a bullet strikes it. Usually rated against Pistol caliber weapons. Provides an additional +3 bonus to AP against Called Shots aimed at the Chest, Heart, or Vitals/Gut.

    Undercover Vest¶

    Armor Protection: +6, Cost: 20 RP

    A Kevlar or ceramic vest designed to fit underneath the clothing for concealment, to stop most pistol caliber rounds, and some shotgun rounds. Provides an additional +3 bonus to AP against Called Shots aimed at the Chest, Heart, or Vitals/Gut.

    Armored Suit¶

    Armor Protection: +2, Cost: 45 RP

    A dress suit made of bullet and cut resistant fabrics. Often utilized by groups such as the US Secret Service, in the protection details of high-value individuals. It provides reasonable enough protection at a minimum cost to mobility. The downside to this is that it cannot be modified, outside of stylistic choices.

    Light Duty Vest¶

    Armor Protection: +6, Cost: 30 RP

    A light Kevlar vest worn on the outside of clothing. Often standard-issue for most police departments. Provides an additional +3 bonus to Defense against Called Shots aimed at the Chest, Heart, or Vitals/Gut.

    Armor Protection: +7, Cost: 40 RP

    A more robust armor system, utilizing replaceable armor plates. This armor is often standard-issue for tactical police units, and military units.     Hazard Protection Armor¶

    Armor Protection: +8, Cost: 45 RP

    A full-body suit of armor that hosts a number of features designed to keep the wearer safe against all manner of contaminants, radiation, lack of atmosphere, environmental hazards such as extreme temperatures.

    Built-in environmental seals and self-cleaning filters make this armor a must-have item for nuclear, biological, and chemical warfare.

    Hazard Protection Armor must be doffed and filters must be allowed a cleaning cycle at least once every 8 hours of use to continue functioning properly.

    Special Response Vest¶

    Armor Protection: +9, Cost: 50 RP

    This armor is made to carry removable plates rated up to high powered rifle rounds. It's been hardened to provide maximum defense by sacrificing mobility. Provides an additional +3 bonus to Defense against Called Shots aimed at the Chest, Heart, or Vitals/Gut.

    Forced Entry Unit¶

    Armor Protection: +12, Cost: 70 RP

    A bulky, heavy armor kit designed for dealing with heavy weapons and explosive devices. Hardened. Provides an additional +4 Armor Protection against damage from all explosive devices. Provides an additional +3 bonus to Defense against all Called Shots.

    Shields¶

    Although not in the same demand as they used to be, shields do still find their way into modern tactical discussion. They provide a mobile barrier, for improved safety during high-risk situations.

    Shields and AP

    Shields give only half their AP as a penalty to Reflexes for determining Speed.

    Tower Shield +4 +8 30 Heavy

    Weapons¶

    Sometimes, words just won't do. When things go bad, you have to pick up a weapon, and get moving. Look for cover, and don't get pinned in.

    Weapon Groups¶

    Weapons in this system are broken into groups. Training and Proficiency with weapon groups is simple. Before you can take the Trait for a bonus to using a certain Weapon Group, you must first be trained in its use. This can be done by spending SP. Whenever you gain training in a weapon group, mark that group on your Character Sheet. As with armor, the weapons listed are mostly generalized and broad. If more specificity is needed, players and GMs should coordinate, and the GM should adjust the RP cost accordingly. That said, the weapon groups and their descriptions are as follows:

    Melee Groups¶

    Archaic Melee:
    Weapons in this category aren't as common in a modern setting, but there are some in the world who still train in their use. This category includes weapons such as: Swords, Spears, and Axes.
    Brawling:
    Weapons in this category are generally items that are worn on or around the hand. This category includes weapons such as: Brass Knuckles, Boxing Gloves, Tactical Gloves, and Hand Wraps. note: Unarmed Attacks, while not technically an item, had to be detailed somewhere.
    Light Melee:
    Light Melee weapons are versatile, meaning they can be used with either Might or Reflexes, and can be used with a single hand, leaving the other free to perform other tasks, such as rude gestures. Most small martial arts weapons are found in this category, such as: Batons, Knives, and Tasers.
    Heavy Melee:
    Heavy Melee weapons are often large and bulky, or require specialized martial training to be utilized. They often require both hands to use properly. Weapons in this category can include: Quarterstaves, Machetes, and Sledgehammers.
    Improvised Melee:
    If it can be lifted and swung, you can likely hit someone with it. These items are likely to be unwieldy or awkward to fight with. The damage these items cause is based on the material they're made from. Add the AP value of the item's material to the damage dice of this weapon. Light Melee Weapons thrown would also be counted in this category.

    Ranged Groups¶

    Archaic Ranged:
    Ranged weapons from a bygone age, now seen mostly for their sport or hunting potential. These are weapons such as: Bows and Crossbows
    Small-Arms:
    Small-Arms can be one or two-handed firearms, but typically feature a lighter frame, and smaller caliber ammunition. Small-Arms are weapons such as: Light Pistols, Submachine Guns, and Heavy Pistols.
    Long-Arms:
    These firearms are longer, larger, and typically fire heavier or more specialized rounds. Weapons in this group are those such as: Rifles and Shotguns.
    Heavy-Arms:
    These weapons tend to be particularly heavy, bulky, or unwieldy; often requiring more specialized training to properly implement in combat. Weapons in this group are those such as: Machine Guns, Grenade Launchers, and Rocket Launchers.
    Improvised Ranged:
    If it can somehow launch a projectile, you know which end to point at an enemy. These are very unusual items, not normally used for the purposes of a standard firearm. The damage caused by these weapons is based on the material the projectile is made from. Add the AP value of the item's material to the damage dice of this weapon.

    Other Groups¶

    Thrown Weapons:
    These weapons are designed to be thrown to cause damage. A lot of them are either heavy, bladed, or otherwise sufficiently dangerous, but difficult to fire out of any kind of launcher. This could include: Javelins, Throwing Knives, etc.
    Explosives:
    Not necessarily a weapon group used in direct contention, although they could be. Explosives are detailed further below, but tend to be those items used in the destruction of materiel.

    Firearms¶

    Listed below are several categories of firearms. There are Small-Arms, most of which are light and can be held and fired safely with one hand. Long-Arms, which are long bodied firearms such as rifles and shotguns, higher powered and useful for a variety of situations. And Heavy-Arms, weapons that have the potential to cause devastation, and are therefore usually restricted to military use.

    There are a few special rules when dealing with firearms. As they are complex mechanical weapons, utilizing miniature explosions and moving parts, there are chances that something could go wrong with it.

    Firearm Malfunctions

    All firearms have a potential for Malfunction. Some more than others. Unless otherwise stated in the weapon's features, firearms will misfire on a roll of 4, and jam on a roll of 3.

    Misfire: Rolling a 4 on 3d6, regardless of doubles, causes a misfire, where for some reason your weapon does not fire. This could be a stuck part on a revolver, or a double feed for a pistol. Misfires require a Minor Action to make a quick check and clear the weapon, making sure it's ready to fire again.

    Jam: Rolling a 3 on 3d6, regardless of doubles, causes the weapon to jam. This could be a bullet casing lodged in the slide, or some other debris preventing the weapon from being fired. Jams require a Major Action to dislodge the obstruction and bring your weapon back into service.

    Crafting¶

    Over the course of a Nomad campaign, a character may or may not be exactly inclined to buy just any run-of-the-mill items. Perhaps they have an engineering background, and have an idea for a new device. Perhaps they simply want to forge their own weapons and armor. Perhaps they wish to concoct medicines or poisons which simply are not available over the counter.

    Whatever the case may be, this Sub-Section of the Equipment Chapter is for those characters who can't leave an item half-finished.

    Creating new items often costs time, money, and materials. How much time, how much money, and how much of what materials all depend on the kind of item you wish to create.

    NOTE

    When crafting, if something you're trying to build is out of the ordinary, check with your GM to consider the item's fit within the campaign setting, or power scaling.

    Core Crafting Mechanic¶

    A character wishing to craft equipment must know something about what they're trying to build. This is represented by a Wits (Design) check with a CR based on the item's tier, as shown in the charts below. This Design check can take several hours to several days, again depending on the Crafting tier of the item in question.

    The character may then roll a Crafting Action Roll with the appropriate Ability (Expertise) combination to the Craft Action Roll. Even if an item is to be crafted over several weeks, this check is only rolled once. On a failed design roll, the item is improperly designed, and cannot be crafted. On a failed craft roll the item is made, but broken beyond repair, and the materials to make it are lost.

    If a character does not have the Expertise associated with the crafting check they are attempting, they roll the crafting check with a Major Disadvantage (-5).

    Info

    One exception to these mechanics is a character crafting a temporary item using Wits (Jury-Rigging). This special circumstance does not require a Design check, but the item crafted will always have complications associated with it. The rules surrounding this special circumstance will be detailed below.

    In the charts below, you'll notice that the Crafting tiers have a range of CRs. This range represents the difficulty in crafting an item to perform exactly as you want it to. Your Design check only needs to exceed the lower CR to properly design an item. If your Craft check meets the lower CR or above, their item is made, but with a number of random complications equal to the character's Chance Die. Meeting the upper CR means the item will function exactly as designed. Finally, exceeding the upper CR will allow an item to not only function exactly as designed, but will also allow the item a number of bonuses equal to the character's Chance Die.

    Basic Crafting is as follows:

    1. The primary crafter rolls their Wits (Design) test. This roll is compared to the Crafting chart for the CR and time spent.

    2. The primary crafter makes the initial crafting roll using their relevant Ability Score and relevant Expertise, if available, and is assumed to be working on their item for the time indicated. If this time is split in any way, they roll another craft check to pick up where they left off.

    Crafting time can be reduced if multiple characters assist with the creation of an item. For each additional character actively involved in the crafting process, reduce the crafting time by 1 step, and add half of that character's relevant Ability Score (rounded down) as a bonus to the primary crafter's roll. The crafting time can only be reduced a maximum of 2 steps, regardless of the number of contributors.

    Crafting Results:

    • Below Minimum CR: You fail to craft the item, and the resources used are consumed. For some standard items, this doesn't mean too much. For items such as poisons and explosives, however, this failure could potentially lead to harm. (See below)

    • Between Minimum and Maximum CR: You successfully craft the item you intended. It's not perfect, however, and is crafted with a number of randomly rolled Complications (See Complications & Bonuses Chart) equal to your Chance Die at the time of your roll.

    • Meeting Maximum CR: You successfully craft the item you envisioned, perfectly. The item now exists, and can be used until its used up, discarded or broken.

    • Above Maximum CR: You successfully craft the item you envisioned, but with a few last minute tweaks. If you exceed the Maximum CR by 2, you may choose a number of Bonuses from the Complications & Bonuses Chart equal to half the value of your Chance Die (Rounded Down), to a minimum of 1 Bonus. If you exceed the Maximum CR by 5, you may choose any number of Bonuses to add, up to the full value of your Chance Die.

    Equipment Damage & Destruction¶

    How This Works¶

    Not all items are created with equal toughness in mind. This sub-chapter presents rules for attacking and damaging items, vehicles, and structures. The purpose for this mechanic is to give another layer of tactical edge to the system as a whole. Items can be targeted by enemy attacks. Buildings and vehicles can be hit by rockets, or demolished with planted explosives. And, most unfortunately, sometimes bullets don't care about what taking cover might mean to you.

    In terms of direct attacks, items and equipment must be targeted via Called Shot for sundering. This Called Shot is made at a Minor Disadvantage. For additional Called Shot rules, see pg. 88. Splash, Blast, or otherwise area affecting attacks usually deal damage to everything within a certain space.

    Any time an item is hit, or if an attack roll would hit a target behind full cover, or if the target is missed by 2 or less, the item or cover's AP soaks up a portion of the damage. Subtract the AP from the attack's damage, representing the absorption of some of the impact. If the damage exceeds the cover or item's AP, any remaining damage goes through to what's behind it.

    Every time the AP of the item or cover in question is breached, the item's AP is reduced by an amount equal to the attacking character's Chance Die. Standard Rounds always go against whatever the current AP is. Armor-Piercing Rounds ignore a portion or bypass AP altogether. Explosives or Acid generally degrade larger areas of items or cover at any given time, potentially destroying it completely.

    Some objects are harder than others to destroy. You can't bring a building down with a rifle, after all. When it comes to structures and hardened materials, even if its AP becomes 0, then without actual demolitions, it cannot be destroyed, and functions at the Quarter Damage Threshold until destroyed with the proper ordnance.

    Some items have thresholds of damaged AP, at which their utility and effectiveness are decreased to the point of malfunctioning or losing functionality altogether.

    AP Threshold Effect
    Full The item works in its optimal condition, with little to no issues.
    Half The item begins malfunctioning, imposing Minor Disadvantage on all rolls associated with the item.
    Quarter Severe malfunctions occur, imposing Major Disadvantage on all rolls associated with the item. The item has a chance to stop working temporarily. If a player rolls doubles while using this item, it ceases to function for a number of rounds equal to the Chance Die.
    0 AP The item is destroyed, too damaged to protect, or is otherwise completely useless.
